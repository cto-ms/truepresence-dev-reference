During the warm months of summer and early autumn, while the Ant labored diligently in the fields, gathering seeds and provisions and storing them carefully in his nest for the winter months to come, the Grasshopper spent his days in idle pleasure. He sat upon the grass in the warm sun, singing merrily and leaping about without care or concern for the future.

The Ant, pausing in his labors, would sometimes speak to the Grasshopper: "Friend, should you not also be gathering food and preparing for the cold season that approaches? Winter will come soon enough, and there will be no food to be found in the frozen ground."

But the Grasshopper would only laugh and wave away the Ant's concern. "Why do you trouble yourself with such dreary thoughts?" he would say. "The sun shines warm upon us now, and food is abundant. There is time enough to concern ourselves with winter when it arrives. For now, let us enjoy the gifts of the season!"

The Ant said nothing more, but continued his steady work, carrying each seed and each grain carefully to his nest, storing them with methodical precision against the time of scarcity.

But when the winter came with its bitter cold and heavy snow, the Grasshopper found himself without shelter or food. Starving and freezing, he made his way to the Ant's nest and begged for assistance. "Please," he cried, "I am dying of hunger and cold. Will you not share some portion of your stores with me?"

The Ant, looking upon the suffering Grasshopper with sadness, replied: "Throughout the abundance of summer and autumn, you chose to make merry while I labored. You refused to prepare, though warned. The prudent creature foresees the future and takes action to ensure safety and survival. The imprudent creature indulges in present pleasure and ignores warnings of future hardship."

Yet the Ant, possessed of mercy, did share a portion of his stores, allowing the Grasshopper to survive the winter. The Grasshopper, humbled and grateful, resolved that henceforth he would practice the virtue of prudence.

Odyssey, Book XII
Open Story in Sanity ↗
As Odysseus sailed toward home, his ship approached the island of the Sirens—supernatural beings whose beautiful song was said to bewitch sailors, causing them to steer toward the rocks and perish. The Sirens' song was irresistible to those who heard it directly; legend claimed that no man who heard their voices could maintain the will to avoid them. Yet Odysseus possessed something more valuable than mere strength: the prudence—the practical wisdom—to devise a solution.

Odysseus did not attempt to navigate by virtue alone, trusting that he could hear the song and simply choose to resist. Instead, he demonstrated prudence through careful planning. He instructed his men to fill their ears with beeswax so they could not hear the song. For himself, he ordered them to tie him tightly to the mast, instructing them not to release him no matter how much he pleaded. This was not cowardice but rather the wisdom to acknowledge his limitations and to structure his environment so that desire could not override his judgment.

When the Sirens' song reached him, Odysseus immediately felt the powerful desire to steer toward them. He strained against his bonds, commanding his men to release him. Yet because of his prudent planning, he remained bound and continued toward home. Homer emphasizes that prudence is the virtue of recognizing potential challenges in advance and establishing structures and safeguards that protect us from our own weakness. Prudence is not assuming we will be strong enough to resist temptation but rather acknowledging our vulnerabilities and taking practical steps to shield ourselves. True prudence recognizes that some challenges are better prevented than faced.

A nobleman's son is considered foolish and incapable of learning anything useful. The father, in desperation, sends him to be educated by three different masters, each for a year. The first master teaches him the language of dogs. The second teaches him the language of birds. The third teaches him the language of frogs.

When the son returns, his father is furious—the boy has wasted three years learning what appears to be nonsense. In rage, he declares that his son must leave the household. The boy, undaunted by his father's anger, travels forth.

Soon, he encounters bandits preparing to rob travelers. But the son understands the language of dogs and learns from them that the bandits hide on a particular road. He warns travelers to avoid this road, saving many lives.

Continuing his journey, he encounters a castle where many birds gather outside the windows. Understanding their language, he learns that the castle holds a hidden treasure beneath a stone in the cellar. He offers to find it and claims a great reward.

At a lake, he learns from the language of frogs that a treasure lies at the bottom of the water. He retrieves it and becomes wealthy. The king, hearing of his extraordinary abilities, invites him to his court. The boy becomes a trusted advisor, using his unique wisdom to serve the kingdom.

Prudence—the wisdom to learn, even when others declare learning useless; to persist, even when mocked; and to apply one's knowledge creatively—elevates the "foolish" boy to nobility. True prudence sees value where others see only folly.

Thomas More's Principled Resignation as Lord Chancellor

In 1532, Thomas More resigned as Lord Chancellor of England rather than endorse Henry VIII's break with the Church, despite the king's favor and his own precarious position. His decision to step down demonstrated prudence through careful deliberation about consequences, values alignment, and recognition that maintaining power at the cost of conscience would prove ultimately destructive.

St. Thomas More
virtueStory.era.en
16th century
Open Story in Sanity ↗
Thomas More was born in 1478 in London and became one of England's most accomplished lawyers and intellectuals. He served as Lord Chancellor of England under King Henry VIII, then resigned and eventually died rather than compromise his conscience. More's life exemplifies prudence—the cardinal virtue of practical wisdom that guides all other virtues. As Lord Chancellor, More occupied one of England's highest offices. He had achieved distinction through legal brilliance and moral integrity. Then Henry VIII decided to divorce Catherine of Aragon and marry Anne Boleyn. The Pope refused to grant annulment, creating a profound crisis. Henry sought to resolve the crisis by breaking England from papal authority, placing himself as head of the Church in England. This change required accepting the king as spiritual leader and accepting the king's authority to annul his marriage. More could not conscientiously accept these innovations. They violated both traditional Church authority and his deeply held convictions about the proper relationship between secular and spiritual power. More's prudence involved recognizing that he could not serve a king bent on these changes in good conscience. Rather than pretend conformity while secretly opposing the king's will, More resigned as Lord Chancellor. This was an extraordinary sacrifice; he gave up the highest position he would ever hold. Yet prudence required acknowledging that the office was incompatible with his conscience. More hoped that by quietly resigning and withdrawing from public life, he could avoid direct confrontation. He tried to live peacefully in retirement, writing and pursuing private study. However, Henry demanded that More actively affirm his new religious policies. More refused both to affirm the changes and to openly condemn them. He maintained silence, hoping to avoid forcing the confrontation that both he and the king understood was inevitable. The king interpreted More's silence as disloyalty. More was arrested and imprisoned in the Tower of London. He was charged with treason for failing to support the king's supremacy over the Church. Faced with execution, More maintained his principles. On his execution day in 1535, he told the court that he died as the king's good servant but God's servant first. His prudence had guided him through impossible circumstances. He had not actively rebelled but had refused to violate conscience to preserve his position and life. He understood that some matters superseded self-interest and survival. More's death shocked England and European Christendom. He was eventually canonized, becoming Saint Thomas More. His example influenced Catholic teaching about the relationship between conscience and authority, between obedience and spiritual conviction. His life demonstrated that prudence sometimes requires accepting severe consequences rather than compromising fundamental principles. More's prudence was not recklessness but careful judgment recognizing that conscience and moral integrity transcended the desire for safety and success. His willingness to die rather than betray fundamental conviction established that prudence includes knowing when to resign, when to withdraw, and when to accept consequences rather than compromise one's integrity.
